## Quality and Compliance in the Close

CPA firms face quality requirements in the close that go beyond simply completing the accounting procedures. The close must be documented, reviewed, and approved in a way that demonstrates professional standards were met.

**The completeness question.** How does the firm know that every required step was performed for every client? A reconciliation that was accidentally skipped is a quality failure. An adjusting entry that was not made because it was forgotten is a reporting error. Completeness requires a mechanism that tracks every step and confirms that none were missed.

**The review gap.** In many firms, the review process is informal: the senior "looks over" the work before reports are issued. What exactly was reviewed? Were specific accounts examined? Were reconciliation differences investigated? Without documented review procedures, the firm cannot demonstrate what was reviewed and how thoroughly.

**The approval chain.** Different engagement types may require different levels of approval. A standard monthly close might require manager review and approval. A close that includes financial statements under the firm's letterhead may require partner sign-off. The approval chain should be defined and enforced, not left to individual judgment.

**The audit trail.** If a question arises about a prior period's close (during an audit, a client inquiry, or a quality review), the firm needs to reconstruct what was done: who performed each step, when it was completed, what was reviewed, and who approved the final product. Without automated documentation, this reconstruction requires searching email, files, and memories.

## What Accounting Close Automation Includes

**Standardized close checklists.** Each client has a close checklist that reflects the full set of required procedures. Checklists are created from templates based on client type, engagement scope, and quality requirements. Every checklist item must be marked complete (or documented as not applicable with a reason) before the close can advance to review.

**Review procedure workflows.** When the preparer completes the close, the review workflow begins. The reviewer receives the checklist, the work products, and the supporting documentation. The review workflow defines what the reviewer must examine: specific accounts, reconciliation details, unusual transactions, and financial statement accuracy. Review findings are documented within the workflow.

**Multi-level approval.** The approval workflow routes the completed close through the required approval chain: reviewer approval, manager approval, and partner sign-off (where required). Each approver sees the work product, the review notes, and the preparer's responses to any review findings. Approval is recorded with timestamp and the approver's identity.

**Compliance documentation.** Every step in the close, from the initial checklist through the final approval, is documented automatically: who performed the task, when it was completed, who reviewed it, what findings were noted, and how they were resolved. This documentation creates a permanent record for each client's close that is available for quality reviews, peer reviews, or audit support.

**Exception management.** When a checklist item cannot be completed normally (a bank statement is not yet available, a client has not responded to a question), the exception is documented with the reason, the impact, and the planned resolution. Exceptions are visible in the close status and carried forward until resolved.

**Period lock.** When the close is approved, the period is locked to prevent changes to the closed period's data without formal authorization. Period lock protects the integrity of the closed financials and ensures that any post-close adjustments follow a documented process.

## Your Questions Answered

**Does this slow down the close process?**
In most cases, the structured process actually accelerates the close because handoffs happen immediately, review procedures are defined (not ad hoc), and approval routing is automatic. The documentation is created as work is performed rather than compiled after the fact, so it does not add a separate documentation step.

**Can we define different quality levels for different client types?**
Yes. Standard clients may have a single-level review. Clients with financial statements under the firm's letterhead may require a more detailed review with partner approval. The checklist and approval workflow are configured per engagement type and quality requirement.

**How does this work with peer review requirements?**
The documentation produced by accounting close automation supports peer review preparation. When a peer reviewer examines your firm's work, the automated documentation provides a clear record of procedures performed, reviews conducted, and approvals given for every engagement selected for review.

**What if the reviewer identifies an issue that requires revision?**
The review workflow includes a revision path. When the reviewer notes a finding, the workflow routes back to the preparer with the specific finding and required action. The preparer addresses the finding and resubmits for review. The revision cycle is documented as part of the close record.
