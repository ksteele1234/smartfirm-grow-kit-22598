## The Tax Practice Analytics Gap

Tax practices generate significant performance data during every season, but that data is rarely analyzed systematically.

**The season blur.** During tax season, there is no time to analyze performance. Every hour is consumed by preparation, review, and filing. Performance questions (how many returns have we processed? are we ahead of or behind last year's pace?) go unanswered until the season is over.

**The post-season gap.** After tax season, the team is exhausted. The desire to analyze how the season went is low. By the time anyone gets around to pulling the data, months have passed and the insights are too late to inform changes for the current year. They become notes for next season, assuming they are remembered.

**The manual compilation problem.** Even when someone decides to analyze performance, the data lives across multiple systems: returns processed in the tax software, revenue in the billing system, time spent in the time tracking platform, leads generated in the CRM. Compiling this data into a meaningful analysis requires manual data gathering that takes hours.

**The decision lag.** Without automated analytics, decisions about pricing, staffing, capacity, and marketing are based on instinct and memory rather than data. Did we need another preparer last season? Were we profitable on those $200 returns? How many clients came from the website? Without analytics, these questions are answered with guesses.

## What Analytics Automation Delivers

**Season performance reports.** Automated reports that track returns completed, revenue earned, average revenue per return, and completion rate against the prior year. Reports can be delivered weekly during season and monthly during off-season, providing a running view of practice performance.

**Capacity and utilization analytics.** Automated calculation of hours worked, returns per preparer, utilization rates, and capacity remaining. These metrics during season help with real-time staffing decisions. Post-season, they inform capacity planning for the next year.

**Revenue and profitability analytics.** Revenue by service type (individual, business, trust), revenue by preparer, and cost per return (factoring in labor and overhead). Profitability analytics identify which return types and which client segments contribute the most to the bottom line.

**Client acquisition analytics.** Lead volume, conversion rates, acquisition cost, and revenue generated by clients from each acquisition channel (website, referrals, advertising). Acquisition analytics inform marketing decisions and help calculate the return on marketing investment.

**Season-over-season comparisons.** Automated year-over-year comparisons for key metrics: total returns, total revenue, average revenue per return, new client volume, and utilization rates. Comparisons reveal whether the practice is growing, stable, or declining across the dimensions that matter.

**Automated delivery.** Reports are generated and delivered automatically on the schedule you define: weekly during season, monthly during off-season, quarterly for strategic metrics. Delivery can be via email, dashboard, or both. The reports arrive without anyone requesting or compiling them.

## Your Questions Answered

**What data do we need to start tracking?**
The data sources depend on which metrics you want to track. At minimum, tax software (returns processed), billing data (revenue), and time tracking (hours worked) provide the foundation for most tax practice analytics. CRM data adds acquisition and pipeline metrics. More data sources enable richer analysis.

**Can we start tracking now and analyze retroactively?**
If historical data is available in your systems (past tax seasons, prior year billing), it can be incorporated into the analytics. The more history available, the more valuable the trend analysis and season-over-season comparisons.

**How does this work if I am a solo practitioner?**
Analytics automation is equally valuable for solo practitioners. In fact, solo preparers often have less visibility into their own practice performance because there is no one else to compile the data. Automated analytics provide the same insights without requiring you to dedicate time to manual analysis.

**Can the analytics help with pricing decisions?**
Yes. Revenue per return, cost per return (including your time), and profitability by return type provide the data needed to evaluate pricing. If your average cost to prepare a business return is $X and your average fee is $Y, you can make informed decisions about whether to adjust pricing, improve efficiency, or change the client mix.
