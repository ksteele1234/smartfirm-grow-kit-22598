# KPI Dashboard for CPAs

A KPI dashboard for CPAs defines, calculates, and displays the key performance indicators that measure your firm's operational and financial health. KPIs are specific, measurable metrics selected because they capture the factors most important to your firm's performance: utilization rate, realization rate, revenue per FTE, client acquisition cost, average revenue per client, billing cycle time, and others relevant to your practice model. A KPI dashboard is not a general data display. It is a curated set of metrics, each chosen because it answers a specific question about how the firm is performing relative to its goals.

## What Makes a KPI Different from a Metric

Every KPI is a metric, but not every metric is a KPI. The distinction matters because a dashboard cluttered with every possible metric loses its power. KPIs are selected based on their ability to indicate performance on the dimensions that matter most to the firm.

**Actionable.** A KPI reveals something you can act on. "Total revenue" is a metric. "Revenue per FTE" is a KPI because it tells you whether adding another team member would be profitable. If revenue per FTE is declining, you may need to improve pricing, reduce overhead, or increase utilization before adding headcount.

**Leading or lagging.** Leading KPIs predict future performance: pipeline value, prospect conversion rate, and utilization trend. Lagging KPIs confirm past performance: annual revenue, client retention rate, and realization rate. A balanced dashboard includes both so you can see where the firm is headed and where it has been.

**Benchmarkable.** KPIs are most useful when they can be compared against a target: your own historical performance, industry benchmarks, or firm goals. A utilization rate of 65% means nothing without context. Compared to the firm's target of 75%, it signals underutilization. Compared to last year's 60%, it signals improvement.

## KPIs for CPA Firms

**Utilization rate.** Billable hours divided by available hours. Measures how much of the team's time is spent on revenue-generating work. Target varies by role: partners may have lower utilization (more business development and management) while staff should have higher utilization.

**Realization rate.** Revenue collected divided by revenue billed at standard rates. Measures how much of the value of work performed is actually captured as revenue. A realization rate below 100% indicates write-downs, discounts, or uncollected fees that erode margins.

**Revenue per FTE.** Total revenue divided by full-time equivalent employees. Measures the revenue productivity of the team. Trends in revenue per FTE indicate whether the firm is scaling efficiently or whether overhead is growing faster than revenue.

**Average revenue per client.** Total revenue divided by client count. Indicates the value of the average client relationship. Declining average revenue per client may indicate that new clients are smaller than existing ones, or that pricing is not keeping pace with scope.

**Client acquisition cost.** Total marketing and business development spend divided by new clients acquired. Measures the cost of acquiring each new client. When compared to client lifetime value, this KPI indicates whether the firm's growth is financially sustainable.

**Billing cycle time.** Average days between work completion and payment receipt. Measures the efficiency of the billing and collection process. Long billing cycle times indicate delayed invoicing, slow collections, or payment terms that could be improved.

**Client retention rate.** Percentage of clients retained year over year. Measures relationship stability. A declining retention rate signals potential service quality, pricing, or competitive issues.

**Engagement completion rate.** Percentage of engagements completed on time relative to deadline. Measures operational reliability. A low completion rate may indicate capacity issues, workflow bottlenecks, or scope management problems.

## How the KPI Dashboard Is Built

**KPI selection.** We work with your firm's leadership to select the KPIs that align with your strategic priorities. A firm focused on growth may emphasize acquisition cost and pipeline metrics. A firm focused on profitability may emphasize realization rate and revenue per FTE. The selection ensures the dashboard tracks what matters most to your firm.

**Calculation definition.** Each KPI's formula is defined precisely: which data fields are included, what time period is measured, and how edge cases are handled (new hires in utilization calculations, mid-year client additions in retention calculations). Precise definitions ensure the numbers are consistent and comparable over time.

**Data source connection.** Each KPI connects to the data sources that provide its inputs: time tracking for utilization, billing for realization and revenue, CRM for acquisition cost and retention, and practice management for engagement completion. Connections pull data automatically so KPIs are always current.

**Dashboard design.** KPIs are displayed in a format that enables quick assessment: current value, trend over time, comparison to target, and status indicator (on target, approaching concern, below target). The design prioritizes clarity and actionability over visual complexity.

## Your Questions Answered

**How many KPIs should we track?**
Most firms find that 8 to 12 KPIs provide sufficient coverage without overwhelming the dashboard. A smaller set of well-chosen KPIs is more useful than a large set that dilutes attention. Additional metrics can be tracked in supplementary reports without cluttering the primary KPI dashboard.

**What if we do not have targets for our KPIs?**
Setting targets is part of the KPI dashboard engagement. For some KPIs, industry benchmarks provide a starting point. For others, your firm's historical performance establishes the baseline. Targets are refined over time as the firm gains experience with its KPI data.

**How is this different from a general data dashboard?**
A general data dashboard displays data from multiple sources. A KPI dashboard is a specific type of dashboard focused exclusively on key performance indicators: curated metrics with defined calculations, targets, and trend tracking. The KPI dashboard is strategic; a general data dashboard may include operational details that support day-to-day management.

**Can we change our KPIs over time?**
Yes. As the firm's strategic priorities evolve, KPIs can be added, removed, or modified. A firm in a growth phase may track different KPIs than one focused on profitability improvement. The dashboard is designed to accommodate changes as the firm's focus shifts.
