## The Real Cost of Manual Intake

Most accounting firms underestimate how much time manual client intake actually consumes. It is not just the initial document request. It is the follow-up emails. The phone calls to clarify incomplete information. The staff time spent organizing documents that arrived in different formats across different channels. The duplicate data entry from intake forms into your practice management software.

For a firm bringing on 50 new clients per year, manual intake can consume hundreds of hours of staff time annually. That is time your team could spend on billable work, client service, or business development.

Beyond the time cost, manual intake creates quality problems:

**Inconsistent information collection.** Different staff members ask for different things in different ways. One person remembers to request the EIN letter, another forgets. The result is incomplete client files that cause problems later.

**Slow turnaround.** When intake depends on manual follow-up, the pace is dictated by how quickly your team remembers to chase missing items. Clients who would have responded within a day to an automated reminder instead wait a week because nobody followed up.

**Poor first impression.** A disorganized intake process tells new clients that your firm may not be as organized as they hoped. First impressions are hard to undo.

## What Automated Client Intake Looks Like

An automated intake system replaces the manual chase with a structured workflow. Here is how it typically works:

**Step 1: Trigger.** When a new client signs an engagement letter (or when a staff member initiates the intake), the system automatically launches the intake workflow.

**Step 2: Welcome and instructions.** The client receives a branded welcome message with clear instructions about what they need to provide and how to provide it. This is delivered automatically, so there is no delay between signing and first contact.

**Step 3: Digital intake form.** The client fills out a questionnaire tailored to their service type. The form collects entity information, contact details, key dates, and any other information your team needs to begin work. Conditional logic ensures they only see questions relevant to their situation.

**Step 4: Document upload.** The client uploads required documents through a secure portal or file request. The system shows them exactly which documents are needed, tracks which ones have been submitted, and marks which are still outstanding.

**Step 5: Automated reminders.** If the client has not completed their intake within a set timeframe, the system sends reminders automatically. These reminders are specific: "We are still waiting for your prior-year tax return and bank statements" rather than a generic "please complete your intake."

**Step 6: Internal notification.** Once the client has submitted everything, your team is notified that the intake is complete and the file is ready for work. If specific items remain outstanding after multiple reminders, the system escalates to a staff member for personal follow-up.

## Integrating Intake With Your Existing Tools

One of the biggest barriers to automation is the fear that it requires replacing your existing systems. It does not. Automated intake works alongside what you already use.

**Practice management software.** Intake data can feed directly into your practice management system so you are not re-entering information manually.

**Document storage.** Uploaded documents can be routed to your existing file structure, whether that is a cloud storage system, a document management platform, or your practice management software's built-in storage.

**CRM or client database.** Contact information collected during intake populates your client records automatically, keeping your database current without manual updates.

**Email.** Automated communications are sent from your firm's email address, so clients see messages from your brand, not from a third-party tool.

The goal is a seamless experience where the automation handles the repetitive work and your existing tools receive the results.

## Your Questions Answered

**How can you automate document collection from clients?**
Automated document collection works through a secure file request system. When intake begins, the client receives a link to a portal showing exactly which documents are needed. They upload each item, the system marks it as received, and your team is notified. If a document is missing after a set period, an automated reminder goes out. This replaces the email chain of "can you send us your W-2?" followed by "just checking in" followed by "still need your W-2."

**Will clients find an automated intake process impersonal?**
The opposite, usually. Clients prefer a clear, organized intake process over a chaotic one. The automation handles the logistics (what to send, where to send it, reminders) while your team handles the personal touches (welcome calls, introductions, answering questions). Clients feel better served when the process is smooth, even when automation is doing the heavy lifting behind the scenes.

**What if our clients are not tech-savvy?**
The intake experience should be simple enough that any client can complete it regardless of technical comfort. That means straightforward forms, clear instructions, and a document upload process that works on any device. For clients who truly cannot use a digital system, the workflow can include a fallback path (mailing documents, in-person drop-off) while still tracking completion through the automated system.

**How does this work with our existing practice management software?**
Integration approach depends on which software you use. Many practice management platforms have built-in intake features or integrate with third-party automation tools. During discovery, we assess your current software and design the intake automation to work with it, not replace it. The goal is to eliminate double data entry and ensure information flows from the intake form into your existing systems automatically.
