## The Trigger-Action Framework

Every repetitive task in your accounting firm follows the same basic pattern: something happens (the trigger), and then someone does something about it (the action). Automation works by replacing the "someone" with a system.

Here are real examples from accounting firms:

**Trigger:** New client signs an engagement letter.
**Manual action:** Staff member sends welcome email, creates client record in practice management system, assigns staff, sends document request.
**Automated action:** System automatically sends branded welcome email, creates the client record, assigns the appropriate team member based on service type, and sends a customized document request.

**Trigger:** Client submits documents through the portal.
**Manual action:** Someone checks the portal, downloads the documents, files them in the client folder, updates the status tracker, and notifies the assigned staff member.
**Automated action:** System detects the upload, files the document in the correct folder, updates the tracking system, and sends a notification to the assigned team member.

**Trigger:** A deadline is approaching.
**Manual action:** Someone reviews the calendar, sends reminder emails to affected clients, updates the internal task list, and alerts the responsible staff members.
**Automated action:** System sends deadline reminders to clients at pre-set intervals, updates internal task status, and alerts staff automatically.

The pattern is the same every time: trigger, action, notification. Once you see it, you start noticing it everywhere in your firm's operations.

## The Most Automatable Tasks in Accounting Firms

Not every task is worth automating. The best candidates share three characteristics: they happen frequently, they follow a consistent pattern, and they do not require professional judgment.

**Client communication workflows.** Welcome emails, appointment reminders, document request follow-ups, deadline notifications, seasonal check-ins, fee reminders, and satisfaction surveys. These happen for every client and follow predictable patterns.

**Status tracking and updates.** When a return moves from "in preparation" to "in review," the system can notify the reviewer, update the client-facing status, and move the task to the next stage automatically. No one needs to manually update a spreadsheet or send an internal email.

**Document management.** When a client uploads a file, the system can recognize the file type, route it to the correct folder, update the document checklist, and notify the team. When a document is missing, the system can send a targeted reminder without staff intervention.

**Billing triggers.** When a service is completed or a milestone is reached, the system can generate an invoice draft, send it for approval, and deliver it to the client. Payment confirmations and overdue notices follow the same trigger-action pattern.

**Internal task creation.** When a new engagement begins, when a deadline approaches, or when a client completes a step in their workflow, the system can automatically create tasks for the appropriate team members.

## The Team Coordination Advantage

For multi-person accounting firms, automation solves a problem that goes beyond time savings: coordination. When three staff members, two managers, and a partner are all involved in different aspects of client service, keeping everyone aligned manually requires constant communication. Emails get missed. Status updates are forgotten. Work gets duplicated.

Automated workflows keep everyone on the same page without requiring anyone to actively manage the coordination:

**Everyone sees the same status.** When the system tracks task progress automatically, there is no ambiguity about where things stand. The partner does not need to ask the manager, and the manager does not need to ask staff.

**Handoffs happen automatically.** When staff finishes a preparation step, the system moves the work to the reviewer. When the reviewer approves, the system routes it for delivery. No one needs to remember to notify the next person in the chain.

**Nothing falls through the cracks.** Automated reminders and escalations ensure that stalled tasks get attention. If a document request has not been fulfilled after two reminders, the system can escalate to a phone call task for the assigned staff member.

## Your Questions Answered

**What tasks should we not automate?**
Do not automate anything that requires professional judgment, nuanced communication, or relationship-building. Tax advisory conversations, complex client questions, scope discussions, and sensitive billing conversations all need a human touch. Automation handles the routine and predictable so your team can focus on the work that requires expertise and empathy.

**How long does it take to set up workflow automation?**
It depends on scope. A single workflow (like automated document request follow-ups) can be built and running quickly. A comprehensive automation system covering multiple workflows across the firm is a larger project. Most firms benefit from starting with one or two high-impact workflows and expanding from there rather than trying to automate everything at once.

**What happens when something goes wrong with an automated workflow?**
Well-designed automation includes monitoring and fallback paths. If an automated email fails to send, the system logs the failure and alerts a staff member. If a client does not respond to automated reminders, the system escalates to human follow-up. The goal is not to remove humans from the process. It is to have humans intervene only when needed rather than for every routine step.

**Do we need to change our software to automate?**
Usually not. Most automation is built on top of your existing tools, connecting them and triggering actions between them. We assess your current technology during discovery and design automation that works with what you have. In some cases, a middleware tool or integration platform is added to connect systems that do not talk to each other natively, but your core software typically stays the same.
