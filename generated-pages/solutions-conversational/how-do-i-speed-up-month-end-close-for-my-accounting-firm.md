# How Do I Speed Up Month End Close for My Accounting Firm

If you want to speed up month-end close, the first question is not "what software should we buy?" It is "where is the time actually going?" Most accounting firms assume the close takes long because the work is complex. In reality, the majority of elapsed time is spent waiting, chasing, and coordinating, not doing the accounting.

## The Close Timeline Audit

To speed up your close, you first need to understand where time is lost. Here is an exercise that reveals the truth:

For your next month-end close, track these timestamps for each client:

- When the month ended
- When you had all documents needed to begin
- When the close work actually started
- When the close work was completed
- When the review was completed
- When the reports were delivered to the client

In most firms, this audit reveals a striking pattern: the gap between "month ended" and "had all documents" is the longest phase. The actual accounting work is a fraction of the total elapsed time.

**Example:** Month ends January 31. Client sends bank statements February 8. You begin the close February 12 (because other clients were ahead in the queue). Close work takes 3 hours, completed February 12. Review happens February 18 (reviewer was busy). Reports delivered February 20.

Total elapsed time: 20 days. Total work time: 4 hours (including review). The other 19.8 days were waiting and queuing.

## The Five Bottlenecks That Slow Every Close

**1. Document availability.** You cannot close what you cannot reconcile, and you cannot reconcile without statements. Every day a client delays sending documents pushes the entire close back. Automated reminders that go out on the first of the month and escalate daily cut this delay significantly.

**2. Information gaps discovered mid-close.** You start the close and realize a transaction needs clarification, a receipt is missing, or an entry does not match. You send an email to the client, then wait. The return might sit untouched for days. Proactive pre-close reviews that identify these gaps before work begins prevent mid-close interruptions.

**3. The review queue.** In many firms, one or two people review all completed closes. This creates a bottleneck where finished work sits in a queue for days waiting for reviewer attention. Distributing review responsibility or scheduling specific review blocks each week can break this logjam.

**4. Report preparation and delivery.** After the close is complete and reviewed, someone needs to generate reports, format them, write commentary, and send them to the client. This step is often treated as low priority and gets pushed until someone gets around to it. Automating report generation and delivery eliminates this final delay.

**5. Lack of a defined close schedule.** Without a deadline for each phase of the close, work expands to fill the available time. Setting specific targets (e.g., "all documents received by the 5th, close complete by the 10th, reports delivered by the 15th") creates urgency and accountability.

## The Continuous Close Approach

The traditional approach treats month-end close as an event: the month ends, and then the closing work begins. A more efficient approach spreads the work throughout the month:

**Daily transaction categorization.** Instead of categorizing a month's worth of transactions after month-end, review and categorize transactions daily or weekly as they flow through bank feeds. By the time month-end arrives, most transactions are already handled.

**Ongoing reconciliation.** Bank reconciliation does not have to wait until the statement is available. You can reconcile cleared transactions throughout the month and complete the final reconciliation quickly once the statement arrives.

**Pre-close review on the 25th.** A few days before month-end, review each client's books for obvious issues: uncategorized transactions, unusual balances, pending items. Addressing these before the month ends means the actual close requires minimal cleanup.

**Same-day close for prepared clients.** When a client's books have been maintained throughout the month, the formal close can happen on the first or second business day of the following month. No two-week lag.

## Your Questions Answered

**What is a realistic close timeline for an accounting firm?**
Best-in-class firms close client books within 5 to 10 business days after month-end. Many firms currently take 15 to 20 business days or more. The achievable timeline depends on client complexity and document availability, but most firms can significantly reduce their current close time with process improvements and automation.

**Should we invest in software to speed up the close?**
Software helps, but process comes first. A firm with a broken close process and great software will still have a slow close. Start by identifying and fixing the bottlenecks (document delays, review queues, manual report generation), then evaluate whether additional software can further improve the process. In many cases, better use of existing tools is the first and most impactful step.

**How do we get clients to send documents faster?**
Make it easy and create urgency. Provide a simple upload link rather than asking clients to email documents. Send automated reminders that specify exactly what is needed. Communicate a clear deadline and explain how late documents affect their reporting timeline. Most clients respond faster when they understand the connection between their document submission and their financial report delivery.

**Does a faster close sacrifice accuracy?**
No. A well-designed close process is actually more accurate than a rushed, ad hoc one. Standardized checklists ensure every step is completed. Pre-close reviews catch issues before they compound. Automation reduces the data entry and manual handling that cause most errors. Speed and quality improve together when the process is right.
