# How to Ask Clients for Reviews as a Tax Preparer

Knowing how to ask clients for reviews is especially important for tax preparers because your client interactions are concentrated into a few intense months. For that short window, you are talking to more people than most businesses talk to all year. Then it is over, and your clients disappear until January.

That seasonal pattern creates both the biggest opportunity and the biggest challenge for building your online reputation. SmartFirm builds a review system designed specifically for the rhythm of tax preparation work.

## The Tax Preparer's Review Window

Your ideal review moment is narrow and specific: right after a client's return is accepted and they see their refund coming. That is peak satisfaction. They are relieved, grateful, and feeling good about the money they just saved or the refund they are about to receive.

Miss that window by even two weeks and the motivation drops dramatically. By summer, they have forgotten the details of the experience entirely.

The problem is that during the exact weeks when your clients are happiest, you are at maximum capacity. You are filing returns, answering questions, and managing deadlines. Asking for reviews is the last thing on your mind.

This is why automation matters more for tax preparers than almost any other professional. The window is short, the volume is high, and your bandwidth is zero.

---

## Why Off-Season Reviews Matter Too

Most tax preparers focus all their marketing energy on January through April. But prospects search for tax preparers year-round. People who just got a notice from the IRS, people who moved to a new city, people who are unhappy with their current preparer and want to switch before next season.

If your most recent review is from April and a prospect finds you in September, that five-month gap creates doubt. A system that generates some reviews during the off-season, from amended returns, quarterly estimate clients, or year-round bookkeeping clients, keeps your profile looking active and current.

---

## Text Messages vs. Emails for Tax Clients

Here is something specific to tax preparation: many of your clients prefer text communication. They text you photos of their W-2s. They text to ask when their return will be filed. They text to confirm their appointment.

If that is how they communicate with you, that is how you should ask for reviews. Text-based review requests have significantly higher open and response rates than email, especially for tax clients who may not check their email regularly outside of work.

A good review system uses whichever channel your client is already on, whether that is text, email, or both.

## Your Questions Answered

**Can I offer a discount on next year's return in exchange for a review?**
You need to be careful here. Offering incentives for reviews is against Google's terms of service and can result in reviews being removed or your listing being penalized. You can offer a discount as a general loyalty perk or referral bonus, but do not tie it directly to leaving a review. The system we build focuses on making the ask easy and well-timed, which is more effective than incentives anyway.

**What if I only see most clients once a year?**
That is exactly why timing matters so much. The automated system triggers a request at the moment of highest satisfaction, right after their return is accepted. You only get one shot per year with most clients, so the system is designed to make that one request count.

**Should I focus on Google or other platforms?**
Google is where it matters. Most prospects search for tax preparation help on Google, and your Google Business Profile is what shows up in local results. Spreading your efforts across multiple platforms dilutes your results. We focus on building a strong Google review profile because that is where the search visibility and client trust are built.

**What about clients who are not tech-savvy?**
This comes up a lot with tax preparers who serve older clients. We can include a short video walkthrough showing how to leave a Google review without a Gmail account, and your front desk staff can offer to walk clients through it in person. Sometimes the personal touch is what it takes.
