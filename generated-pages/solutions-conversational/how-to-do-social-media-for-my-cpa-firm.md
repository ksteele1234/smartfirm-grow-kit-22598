## The Real Problem Is Not Social Media. It Is Time.

Most CPAs who come to us have tried social media at least once. They set up a LinkedIn profile, posted a few times, maybe shared an article about tax changes. Then busy season hit and they disappeared for four months. When they came back, the algorithm had forgotten they existed.

This cycle repeats because social media demands consistency, and consistency is the one thing a CPA's schedule cannot provide. Your workload is inherently uneven. January through April is relentless. Extension season adds another wave. Year-end planning fills the fall. The windows where you have time to think about marketing are narrow and unpredictable.

The solution is not working harder at social media. It is building a system that produces content on a steady schedule whether you are busy or not.

## Which Platform Matters Most for CPAs?

This is the first question most CPAs ask, and the answer depends on who you want to reach.

**LinkedIn** is the strongest platform for CPA firms targeting business owners, executives, and other professionals. The audience is already in a business mindset. Content about tax strategy, financial planning, and business advisory topics performs well because that is what LinkedIn users expect to see.

**Facebook** is more effective for firms serving individuals, families, or very small local businesses. The audience is broader but less targeted. Facebook works best for community-oriented practices and local visibility.

**X (formerly Twitter)** and **Instagram** have limited value for most CPA firms. Unless you serve a niche that is active on those platforms (creative professionals, tech startups), the effort-to-return ratio is poor.

If you serve business clients and only have bandwidth for one platform, start with LinkedIn. If you serve individual tax clients in a local market, Facebook may serve you better. The mistake is trying to be active everywhere and doing all of them poorly.

## What to Post (and What Not to Post)

CPA firms overthink social media content. You do not need to be clever or entertaining. You need to be useful and consistent.

**Content that works for CPAs:**

- Tax law changes and what they mean for business owners (not just the news, but your interpretation of it)
- Year-end planning reminders with specific action items
- Common mistakes you see clients make and how to avoid them
- "Did you know" posts about deductions, credits, or filing rules most people overlook
- Brief case studies describing a problem you solved (anonymized, of course)

**Content to avoid:**

- Generic motivational quotes that have nothing to do with accounting
- Overly technical posts that only other CPAs would understand
- Anything that could be interpreted as specific tax advice for the reader (liability risk)
- Selling language like "Call now for a free consultation" in every post

The goal is to demonstrate expertise, not to close deals directly from social media. When a prospect sees your content regularly and then needs a CPA, you are already on their short list.

## Your Questions Answered

**Which social media platform is best for CPA firms?**
For most CPA firms targeting business clients, LinkedIn delivers the highest return. The platform's audience is already thinking about business decisions, which makes content about tax planning, advisory services, and financial strategy naturally relevant. If your practice primarily serves individuals or families, Facebook's local targeting can be more effective. We assess your client base during discovery and recommend the platform that fits your practice.

**How often should a CPA firm post on social media?**
Two to three times per week is a strong starting point for most CPA firms. Consistency matters more than volume. Posting twice a week every week for a year produces better results than posting daily for two months and then going silent. We build a posting cadence that is sustainable and adjust it based on engagement data.

**What if our partners do not want to be on social media?**
That is common and completely fine. A firm-level LinkedIn page or Facebook page can carry the strategy. That said, posts from personal profiles (especially partners) tend to get significantly more engagement than company page posts on LinkedIn. We can create a system where content is drafted for partners to share with minimal effort on their part.

**Can social media actually bring in CPA clients?**
Social media rarely produces direct "I saw your post and called" conversions. Its value is in staying visible. When a business owner decides they need a new CPA, they are more likely to contact someone whose name they have seen regularly than a stranger from a Google search. Social media builds the familiarity that makes your other marketing channels more effective.
