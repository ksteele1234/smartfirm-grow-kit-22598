## Quality Over Quantity: What Actually Matters

There is a common misconception that the goal is to get as many reviews as possible as fast as possible. That approach backfires.

Google actively detects review manipulation. Businesses that get a sudden spike of reviews in a short period often see those reviews filtered or delayed. Worse, a profile full of vague one-line reviews ("Great service!") does not build the trust you need with prospects.

What works better:

**Detailed, specific reviews.** A review that says "They helped us migrate from manual spreadsheets to automated monthly reconciliation and saved us 8 hours per month" is worth more than ten reviews that say "Highly recommend."

**Recent reviews.** A prospect cares more about your last 6 months of reviews than your overall count. Five detailed reviews from this year outweigh twenty generic reviews from 2023.

**Consistent pacing.** One or two new reviews per month signals an active, engaged practice. A burst of 15 reviews in one week followed by silence looks artificial.

## The Ethics of Review Generation

If you are searching for how to "generate" 5 star reviews, you should know what crosses the line:

**Never acceptable:** Fake reviews, paying for reviews, writing reviews on behalf of clients, creating fake accounts. Samsung was fined over $340,000 for building fake reviews. In New York, Operation Clean Turf resulted in fines totaling over $350,000 to 19 companies for posting fake online reviews.

**Acceptable and effective:** Making it easy for genuinely satisfied clients to share their experience. Timing your requests well. Following up once or twice. Responding to every review you receive.

The good news is that when you deliver quality work, ethical review generation works. You do not need tricks. You need a system.

## Responding to Reviews Is Half the Strategy

Most accounting practices focus entirely on getting reviews and ignore what happens after. That is a mistake.

Businesses that respond to reviews consistently receive 12% more reviews overall. Responding signals to both Google and prospects that you are engaged and attentive.

**For positive reviews:** Thank the client by name, reference something specific about the work, and keep it brief. This encourages others to leave reviews because they see that their feedback will be acknowledged.

**For critical reviews:** Respond professionally, acknowledge the concern, and offer to discuss it offline. Prospects reading your reviews will judge you more by how you handle criticism than by the criticism itself. A thoughtful response to a negative review can actually build more trust than another 5 star rating.

53% of consumers expect a business to reply to their review within seven days. Having a system that notifies you of new reviews and provides response templates makes this manageable.

## Your Questions Answered

**How do we get clients to write detailed reviews instead of just "Great firm"?**
The review request messaging matters. Instead of "Please leave us a review," the system prompts clients with a specific question like "What changed most about your financial management after working with us?" This kind of prompt naturally produces more detailed, useful reviews.

**Is it OK to ask clients to update old reviews?**
You should not ask clients to change or delete existing reviews. However, if a client's experience has improved since an older review, you can let them know they are welcome to add a new review reflecting their current experience. Most platforms allow multiple reviews from the same person over time.

**Should we set up a review page on our website?**
Yes. A dedicated "Leave a Review" page on your website with a direct link to your Google Business Profile gives clients a clear path. It also gives you a single URL to share in emails, on business cards, and in follow-up communications.

**What if we get a review that mentions confidential information?**
This happens occasionally. Most review platforms allow you to flag reviews that contain sensitive information for removal. Respond to the review thanking the client but note that you cannot discuss specific details publicly, and contact the client directly to ask if they would like to edit the review. Having a plan for this before it happens avoids a scramble.
