## The First 30 Days Define the Relationship

When a new client signs an engagement letter, they are in a window of maximum enthusiasm. They chose your firm. They are ready to get started. What happens in the next 30 days either reinforces that decision or starts eroding it.

Here is what typically goes wrong:

**The handoff from sales to service is messy.** A partner closes the deal and hands the client to a staff member who does not know the full context of the conversations that led to the engagement. The client has to repeat themselves. Information falls through the cracks.

**Document collection becomes a drawn-out chase.** You send the client a list of what you need. They send some of it. You follow up. They send more, but in the wrong format. Two weeks later you are still missing items. The client gets frustrated. Your team gets frustrated.

**Expectations are unclear.** The client does not know what your process looks like, what the timeline is, or what you need from them at each stage. They feel like they are in the dark, and uncertainty breeds dissatisfaction.

**There is no welcome experience.** The client signs the engagement letter and then hears nothing for a week until someone emails them a document request. No welcome message, no introduction to their team, no overview of how the relationship will work.

Every one of these problems is preventable with a structured onboarding process.

## What Should Be Included in a Client Onboarding Packet?

A CPA firm's onboarding packet should accomplish three things: collect what you need, set expectations clearly, and make the client feel confident in their decision. Here is what that typically looks like:

**A welcome message** that thanks the client for choosing your firm, introduces the team members they will work with, and provides a clear overview of what happens next.

**The engagement letter** if not already signed, delivered electronically for easy signature and automatic filing.

**An information questionnaire** tailored to the client's service type. A new tax client needs different information than a new audit client or a new advisory client. The questionnaire should ask for everything you need upfront so you are not chasing missing details later.

**A document checklist** specific to their engagement. Rather than a generic list of "things we might need," provide the specific documents required for their situation with clear instructions on how to submit them.

**A timeline** that tells the client when they will hear from you, when key milestones will happen, and what you need from them by when.

**Communication preferences.** Ask how they prefer to be contacted, who the primary point of contact is, and whether there are any scheduling constraints your team should know about.

The key is that all of this is delivered as a cohesive, branded experience rather than a scattered series of emails sent by different people over several days.

## The Multi-Service Onboarding Challenge

CPA firms that offer tax, advisory, bookkeeping, and other services face a unique onboarding complexity: different services require different information, different timelines, and different team members.

A client engaging your firm for tax preparation, quarterly advisory meetings, and bookkeeping needs three parallel onboarding tracks. The tax track needs prior returns and entity information. The bookkeeping track needs accounting system access and bank feeds. The advisory track needs financial goals and business planning context.

Without a system, these tracks get tangled. The client receives overlapping document requests from different staff members. Some items are asked for twice. Others are missed entirely. The experience feels chaotic even when the underlying work is excellent.

A well-designed onboarding system routes clients through the appropriate tracks based on their engagement scope, coordinates the requests into a coherent sequence, and ensures nothing is missed regardless of how many services are involved.

## Your Questions Answered

**How long should the client onboarding process take?**
For most CPA firm engagements, the goal is to have the client fully onboarded within two to four weeks of signing the engagement letter. The actual timeline depends on how quickly the client provides documents and information. A good onboarding system includes automated reminders that keep the process moving without requiring your team to manually follow up on every missing item.

**What if different partners have different onboarding preferences?**
This is common in multi-partner firms. The onboarding system can accommodate partner-specific preferences (communication style, document requirements, initial meeting format) while maintaining a consistent baseline experience for the client. The key is agreeing on minimum standards that every client receives and allowing customization beyond that.

**Should we onboard clients differently during busy season?**
Your onboarding process should work the same year-round, which is exactly why automation matters. During busy season, your team has the least bandwidth for manual onboarding tasks. An automated system ensures new clients receive the same quality experience in February as they do in July, without adding to your team's workload.

**What documents should clients provide before starting with a CPA firm?**
The specific documents depend on the engagement type, but generally include: prior-year tax returns (for tax clients), entity formation documents, current financial statements, access credentials for accounting software, and identification documents for compliance purposes. The most effective approach is a service-specific checklist that asks only for what is relevant to each client's engagement rather than a generic master list.
