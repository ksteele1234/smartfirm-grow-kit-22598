## The Hidden Manual Work in CPA Firms

Every CPA firm has a collection of manual processes that have accumulated over time. Some were created as workarounds when software could not handle a task. Some were inherited from a partner who left years ago. Some exist because "that is how we have always done it." Here are the most common ones:

**Client communication.** Sending appointment reminders, requesting documents, following up on missing information, confirming receipt of submitted materials, sending engagement letters. Each of these involves someone on your team composing or sending an email, tracking whether the client responded, and following up if they did not.

**Data transfer between systems.** Information entered into your CRM gets re-entered into your practice management system. Client data from intake forms gets manually typed into tax software. Billing information from timesheets gets transferred to invoicing. Each re-entry takes time and introduces errors.

**Internal task management.** Tracking which returns are in progress, which need reviewer attention, which are waiting on client information, and which are ready for delivery. In many firms, this tracking happens through a combination of spreadsheets, emails, sticky notes, and "ask the manager."

**Scheduling.** Back-and-forth emails to find a time that works for a client meeting. Manually blocking calendar time for each appointment. Sending confirmation emails and reminder emails before the meeting.

**Billing and collections.** Generating invoices from timesheets, sending them to clients, tracking which have been paid, and following up on overdue accounts. Each step involves manual effort that repeats for every client, every billing cycle.

None of these tasks require a CPA's expertise. All of them consume CPA-level billing time.

## The Billable Hour Cost

Here is the calculation that most CPA firms have never done: add up the hours your team spends on manual administrative tasks per week, then multiply by your average billing rate.

If a staff accountant spends 8 hours per week on tasks that could be automated, and their billing rate is $150 per hour, that is $1,200 per week in lost billable capacity. Over a year, that is $62,400 from a single person. In a firm with five staff members, the number quickly reaches six figures.

This does not mean automation pays for itself overnight. But it reframes the conversation from "can we afford automation?" to "can we afford not to automate?"

## Where to Start: The Automation Audit

You do not need to automate everything at once. The most effective approach is identifying the highest-impact targets first:

**High frequency, low complexity.** Tasks that happen many times per day or week and follow a predictable pattern. Appointment reminders, document request follow-ups, and invoice generation are classic examples. These are the easiest to automate and produce the fastest time savings.

**High time cost, high error rate.** Tasks that consume significant time and are prone to mistakes when done manually. Data transfer between systems is the primary example. Automation eliminates both the time and the errors.

**Client-facing communication.** Any routine client communication that follows a pattern (welcome emails, status updates, deadline reminders) can be automated while still feeling personal. This improves client experience and frees your team simultaneously.

**Low frequency, high stakes.** Tasks that happen less often but have significant consequences if done incorrectly (engagement letter delivery, compliance deadline tracking). These benefit from automation because the system never forgets a step, even during busy season when your team is stretched thin.

Start with the first category. Automate the high-frequency, low-complexity tasks. The time savings from these alone often justify the entire automation investment.

## Your Questions Answered

**Which manual tasks should we automate first?**
Start with high-frequency, low-complexity tasks that follow a predictable pattern. Appointment reminders, document request follow-ups, and client status updates are typically the best starting points because they consume the most aggregate time and are straightforward to automate. From there, move to data transfer automation between your core systems, which reduces both time and error rates.

**Will automation replace our staff?**
Automation replaces tasks, not people. The goal is to free your team from repetitive work so they can focus on client-facing, revenue-generating activities. Most firms that automate do not reduce headcount. They increase capacity, allowing the same team to serve more clients or provide higher-value services.

**How do we get staff to adopt new automated workflows?**
Adoption is easiest when automation clearly makes people's jobs better, not more complicated. Start with automations that eliminate tasks your team already dislikes (data entry, follow-up emails, invoice chasing). When staff see that the system handles the tedious work reliably, they become advocates for expanding automation to other areas.

**What if our systems do not integrate with each other?**
Most modern accounting, practice management, and CRM systems offer integration options, either natively or through middleware tools. During discovery, we assess your current technology stack and identify the best integration approach. Even systems that do not connect directly can often be linked through automation platforms that bridge the gap.
