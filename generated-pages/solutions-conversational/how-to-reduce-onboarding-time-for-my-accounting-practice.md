# How to Reduce Onboarding Time for My Accounting Practice

If it takes your accounting practice more than two weeks to fully onboard a new client, you have a process problem, not a people problem. Finding ways to reduce onboarding time is not about rushing. It is about eliminating the delays, redundancies, and bottlenecks that stretch a process that should take days into one that takes weeks or longer.

## Where Onboarding Time Actually Goes

When accounting firms map their onboarding process, they are often surprised by how much time is spent waiting rather than working. The actual labor involved in setting up a new client (entering their information, creating the engagement, configuring their accounts) might take an hour. But the elapsed time from signed engagement to "ready to serve" stretches to weeks because of these delays:

**Waiting for the client to return documents.** You send a request. The client is busy. Three days pass. You follow up. They send some documents. You realize they missed two items. You follow up again. Another three days. This back-and-forth is the single biggest time sink in most onboarding processes.

**Internal handoffs without clear ownership.** The partner who closed the deal hands the client to a manager, who delegates document collection to a staff accountant, who needs input from the admin team for billing setup. Each handoff introduces a delay. If nobody owns the entire process, things fall through cracks.

**Sequential tasks that could be parallel.** Many firms run onboarding as a linear sequence: first collect information, then send engagement letter, then request documents, then set up billing, then assign staff. But most of these can happen simultaneously. The engagement letter can go out the same day as the document request. Billing setup can happen while documents are being collected. Running tasks in parallel compresses the timeline significantly.

**Manual data entry between systems.** Client information collected on an intake form gets manually entered into the practice management system, then again into the billing system, then again into the tax software. Each re-entry takes time and introduces the risk of errors.

## The Bottleneck Audit

Before you can reduce onboarding time, you need to know where the delays are. Here is a simple way to audit your process:

**Track the actual timeline.** For your next 10 new clients, note the date of each onboarding milestone: engagement signed, welcome sent, documents requested, documents received, engagement letter returned, billing set up, staff assigned, first work started. Calculate the elapsed time between each milestone.

**Identify the longest gaps.** In most firms, the biggest gaps are between "documents requested" and "documents received" and between "handoff to service team" and "first work started." These are your primary targets for improvement.

**Quantify the internal time.** Add up the total staff hours spent on onboarding activities for each client. Compare this to the elapsed calendar time. If a client takes 15 calendar days to onboard but only 2 hours of actual staff time, the process is 95% waiting.

**Look for repeated tasks.** If information is entered more than once, if the same question is asked in the intake form and again during the first meeting, or if multiple people email the client about different onboarding items, you have redundancies that can be eliminated.

## Five Ways to Cut Onboarding Time

**1. Automate document collection and reminders.** Replace manual follow-up emails with an automated system that sends the initial request, tracks what has been received, and sends targeted reminders for specific missing items. This alone typically cuts the document collection phase in half.

**2. Run tasks in parallel.** Send the engagement letter, document request, and intake questionnaire simultaneously instead of sequentially. Set up billing and assign staff while documents are being collected. There is no reason to wait for one step to finish before starting another if they are independent.

**3. Assign a single owner.** One person should own the onboarding process for each new client and be responsible for the timeline. This does not mean they do all the work. It means they track progress, escalate delays, and ensure nothing stalls.

**4. Use digital signatures and forms.** If you are still mailing engagement letters or asking clients to print, sign, and scan documents, you are adding days to the process. Digital signature tools and online forms eliminate this friction entirely.

**5. Integrate your systems.** When data entered in the intake form flows automatically into your practice management, billing, and tax software, you eliminate the manual re-entry steps that slow the process and introduce errors.

## Your Questions Answered

**How can you cut client onboarding time in half?**
The fastest path is automating document collection (which is where most delays live) and running independent tasks in parallel instead of sequentially. Most firms find that just these two changes cut elapsed onboarding time significantly. The specific reduction depends on your current process, but firms going from a fully manual system to an automated one routinely see dramatic improvements.

**Should accounting firms assign a dedicated resource for client onboarding?**
It depends on volume. Firms that onboard more than a few clients per month benefit from having someone own the onboarding function, even if it is not their only responsibility. The key is clear accountability: someone who monitors progress, follows up on stalled onboarding, and ensures every new client completes the process within the target timeline.

**When should an accounting firm start the onboarding process with a new client?**
Immediately after the client commits. The engagement letter, welcome message, intake form, and document request should all be triggered as soon as possible. Every day of delay between "client says yes" and "onboarding begins" is a day the client's enthusiasm cools and the risk of second-guessing increases. Automation makes immediate initiation possible without requiring a staff member to be available at the exact moment the client signs.

**What if our clients are slow to respond regardless of reminders?**
Some clients will always be slow. The goal is not perfection but improvement. Automated reminders with specific, actionable requests ("we are still waiting for your prior-year tax return, which you can upload here") perform significantly better than generic follow-ups. For persistently unresponsive clients, the system can escalate to a personal phone call. The combination of automated reminders and human follow-up as a last resort handles the vast majority of cases.
