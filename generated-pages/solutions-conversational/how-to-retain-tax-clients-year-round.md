## The Seasonal Retention Gap

Tax preparation has a retention problem that most other professional services do not have. A bookkeeper talks to their clients monthly. A financial advisor reviews portfolios quarterly. But a tax preparer who only handles returns might go 10 months without a single interaction with their clients.

During those 10 months, everything is working against you:

**Memory fades.** By October, some clients barely remember your name. They remember the experience was fine, but "fine" does not create loyalty when a competitor's advertisement shows up on their Facebook feed.

**Competitors fill the gap.** Other preparers, tax software companies, and online services are marketing year-round. If you go silent after April, they are the ones staying visible while you are not.

**Life changes happen.** Clients move, change jobs, start businesses, or experience financial events. If you are not in contact when these changes happen, you miss the opportunity to help, and the client may seek guidance elsewhere.

**The client's tax situation changes.** A client who had a simple W-2 return last year might have started freelancing, bought rental property, or moved to a new state. Without year-round communication, you will not know about these changes until they show up at the appointment, if they come back at all.

## The Year-Round Engagement Calendar

The most effective way to stay connected with tax clients between seasons is a planned communication calendar. Here is a practical framework built around the tax year:

**May through June: Post-season follow-up.** Send a thank-you message and a brief summary of key items from their return. Include a reminder about estimated tax payments if applicable. This is the touchpoint most preparers skip, and it sets the tone for year-round engagement.

**July through August: Mid-year financial check-in.** A brief email or text asking if anything significant has changed in their financial situation. New job? Home purchase? Business income? This positions you as someone who pays attention, not just someone who shows up in January.

**September through October: Year-end planning content.** Share tips for reducing tax liability before December 31. Even clients with simple returns appreciate knowing about retirement contribution deadlines, charitable giving strategies, or estimated payment reminders.

**November through December: Pre-season outreach.** Remind clients to gather their documents. Share your availability and booking calendar. Offer early-bird scheduling. Clients who book early are far less likely to drift to a competitor in January.

**January through April: Active season.** During tax season, your communication shifts to operational: appointment reminders, document requests, filing status updates. But the relationship was maintained all year, so clients arrive feeling connected rather than cold.

## Expanding Beyond Returns

The most powerful retention strategy for tax preparers is offering more than just an annual return. This does not mean you need to become a full-service CPA. Small additions can dramatically change how clients perceive your value:

**Estimated tax payment reminders.** Many self-employed clients struggle to remember quarterly payment deadlines. An automated reminder in March, June, September, and January is a high-value, low-effort service that keeps you in regular contact.

**Tax planning conversations.** A 15-minute call in November to discuss whether a client should accelerate income, defer expenses, or make additional retirement contributions turns a once-a-year transaction into an advisory relationship.

**Life event guidance.** When a client tells you they are getting married, having a child, buying a home, or starting a business, a brief explanation of the tax implications positions you as a trusted advisor for the long term.

Each of these touchpoints makes the client relationship stickier. A client who relies on you for quarterly reminders and year-end planning advice is not going to switch preparers over a $50 price difference.

## Your Questions Answered

**How often should tax professionals contact clients throughout the year?**
A monthly or bi-monthly touchpoint is a good baseline. This does not mean a phone call every month. A mix of email newsletters, text reminders, and occasional personal outreach keeps you visible without being intrusive. The key is that clients hear from you regularly enough that your name is familiar when January arrives.

**When should tax preparers start reaching out before tax season?**
October is the ideal time to begin pre-season communication. Start with year-end tax planning content, then transition to document checklists and booking reminders in November and December. By the time January arrives, your returning clients have already gathered their documents and booked their appointments, giving you a head start on the season.

**Should tax preparers offer loyalty incentives to returning clients?**
Small gestures can reinforce loyalty, but they do not need to be discounts. Early-bird booking priority, a brief year-end planning call included with their return, or a simple "thank you for being a returning client" message can be more effective than a price discount. Discounting fees trains clients to expect lower prices and compresses your margins. Adding value is better than reducing price.

**What if I lose clients to TurboTax or other DIY software?**
Clients who leave for DIY software typically fall into two categories: those with genuinely simple returns (single W-2, standard deduction) who may not be your ideal clients, and those who underestimate their tax complexity and will eventually come back after making mistakes. Year-round engagement that demonstrates your knowledge and the complexity of their specific situation helps prevent the second group from leaving in the first place.
