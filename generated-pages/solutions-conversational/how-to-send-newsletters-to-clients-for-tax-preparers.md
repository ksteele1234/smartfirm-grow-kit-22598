# How to Send Newsletters to Clients for Tax Preparers

Figuring out how to send newsletters to clients consistently is one of the most effective things a tax preparer can do to prevent client churn. Your biggest business risk is not losing clients to a competitor during tax season. It is losing them during the 8 months of silence between April and January, when they forget you exist and a coupon from a tax chain catches their eye.

## The 8-Month Silence Problem

Here is how most tax preparer-client relationships work:

**January through April:** Intense communication. Emails, calls, document exchanges. The client feels connected to you and your service.

**May through December:** Silence. No communication unless the client initiates it (usually because of an IRS notice or a question about estimated taxes). For 8 months, your client hears nothing from you.

During those 8 months, your competitors are not silent. The tax chains are advertising. Online tax software is running campaigns. Other preparers are publishing content. Your client is passively absorbing those messages while yours is absent.

A monthly newsletter during the off-season keeps your name in front of clients so that when January arrives, there is no question about who is doing their taxes.

## What to Write About When It Is Not Tax Season

Tax preparers often resist newsletters because they think "what would I even write about in July?" More than you think:

**Q1 (January through March):** Tax filing tips, document checklists, deadline reminders, common mistakes to avoid. This is your highest-relevance period.

**Q2 (April through June):** Post-filing tips, estimated tax payment reminders for those who owe quarterly, summer financial planning for business owners, IRS notice season guidance.

**Q3 (July through September):** Mid-year tax check-in, back-to-school education credits, September estimated payment reminder, small business quarterly review prompt.

**Q4 (October through December):** Year-end tax planning strategies, charitable giving deadlines, retirement contribution reminders, "what to gather for your tax appointment" preparation guide.

Each newsletter should be short. Three to five minutes to read. One main topic, one or two secondary tips, and a reminder of how to reach you. Your clients are not looking for a treatise on tax law. They want useful, bite-sized information that makes their financial lives easier.

## Newsletters as a Client Retention Tool

The economics of client retention for tax preparers are simple: acquiring a new client costs significantly more than keeping an existing one. Every client who does not return next year is a client you need to replace through marketing or referrals.

Newsletters prevent churn by:

**Maintaining the relationship.** Regular communication creates a sense of ongoing relationship even when you are not actively doing tax work for the client.

**Demonstrating year-round value.** When you send a helpful estimated tax reminder in June, the client thinks "my tax preparer is looking out for me even in the off-season." That builds loyalty.

**Creating referral opportunities.** Clients who receive valuable content are more likely to forward it to someone who might need a tax preparer. Every newsletter is a passive referral tool.

**Preventing competitor poaching.** A client who hears from you monthly is significantly less likely to switch preparers than one who has not heard from you since April.

## Your Questions Answered

**I do not have time to write a newsletter every month.**
That is exactly why done-for-you exists. We handle the content creation, design, and sending. You review and approve before it goes out. Your time commitment per month is a few minutes of review, not hours of writing.

**What if my clients do not read newsletters?**
Industry data shows that accounting-related emails get roughly 20 to 25% open rates. That means one in four or five clients reads each newsletter. Over 12 months, the cumulative effect is significant. Even clients who do not open every email see your name in their inbox, which reinforces awareness.

**Should I send different content to individual filers and business clients?**
Yes. Segmentation significantly improves relevance. Business clients care about quarterly estimated taxes, payroll deadlines, and year-end planning. Individual filers care about deduction tips, filing deadlines, and life event tax implications. Sending the right content to the right group improves engagement.

**What if a client asks to be removed from the list?**
Honor unsubscribe requests immediately. It is both legally required and good practice. An unsubscribe does not mean the client is unhappy with your service. Some people simply do not want email. They may still return for tax season. Never take it personally and never re-add someone who unsubscribed.
