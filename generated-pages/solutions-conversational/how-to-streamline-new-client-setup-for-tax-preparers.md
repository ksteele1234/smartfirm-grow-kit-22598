# How to Streamline New Client Setup for Tax Preparers

Finding ways to streamline new client setup is critical for tax preparers, especially during the January-through-April window when most new clients arrive. You might bring on 20, 50, or even 100 new clients in the span of a few months. ## The Seasonal Onboarding Crush

Tax preparers face an onboarding challenge that no other accounting professional deals with: nearly all of your new clients show up at the same time. A bookkeeper might onboard two or three new clients per month throughout the year. A tax preparer might onboard dozens in January and February alone.

This creates a bottleneck that cascades through the entire season:

**New clients arrive unprepared.** They call to make an appointment but have not gathered their documents. They are not sure what they need. They expect you to tell them, one at a time, during your busiest weeks.

**Prior-year returns are hard to obtain.** Clients switching from another preparer need to provide their prior-year returns. Some do not have them. Some need to request them from their previous preparer. Some need IRS transcripts. Each of these situations requires different handling.

**Engagement letters pile up.** Every new client needs a signed engagement letter before work begins. Sending, tracking, and chasing signatures one at a time during peak season is a time sink.

**Information gaps surface during preparation.** A client drops off their documents, you start the return, and you discover that key information is missing. Now you need to stop, contact the client, wait for a response, and pick up the return again later. This start-and-stop pattern destroys productivity.

The solution is getting ahead of these problems with a structured setup process that runs before the client's first appointment.

## The Pre-Appointment Onboarding Process

The most effective tax preparer onboarding happens before the client walks through your door or logs onto a video call. Here is the framework:

**As soon as the client books an appointment**, they should receive an automated welcome message with three things: confirmation of their appointment details, a document checklist specific to their situation, and a secure way to submit those documents in advance.

**The document checklist should be smart, not generic.** A W-2 employee with a mortgage and kids needs a different list than a self-employed contractor with a home office. Asking a few qualifying questions upfront (filing status, income sources, major life changes) lets you generate a checklist that is relevant to their situation. This reduces confusion and increases the chance they submit everything the first time.

**Prior-year return handling should be proactive.** If the client is new to your practice, prompt them to upload their prior-year return or authorize you to obtain an IRS transcript. If they need to request it from their previous preparer, provide instructions immediately. Do not wait until the appointment to discover this gap.

**The engagement letter should go out digitally with the welcome message** so the client can sign it at their convenience. By the time they arrive for their appointment, the administrative work is already done.

## What Should Be Included in a Tax Preparer Onboarding Checklist?

A good onboarding checklist for tax preparers covers these areas:

**Personal identification.** Photo ID and Social Security numbers for the client and all dependents. You will need these regardless of the return type.

**Income documents.** W-2s, 1099s (all types), K-1s, Social Security benefit statements, pension distributions. The specific list depends on the qualifying questions asked upfront.

**Deduction and credit support.** Mortgage interest statements (1098), student loan interest (1098-E), tuition statements (1098-T), charitable contribution records, childcare provider information, health insurance forms (1095-A/B/C).

**Business information (if applicable).** Profit and loss statement, mileage log, home office measurements, business asset purchases, 1099-NEC or 1099-K forms received.

**Prior-year return.** Essential for new clients, especially for verifying prior-year AGI for e-filing and understanding their tax history.

**Banking information.** For direct deposit of refunds or direct debit of payments.

The checklist should be specific and actionable, not a generic "bring all your tax documents." Clients respond better when they can check items off a clear list.

## Your Questions Answered

**What should be included in a tax preparer onboarding checklist?**
The checklist should cover personal identification, all relevant income documents, deduction and credit support documents, business information if applicable, the prior-year return, and banking information for refund processing. The most effective checklists are tailored to each client's situation based on a few qualifying questions, so a W-2 employee does not receive a checklist asking for business profit and loss statements.

**How do I handle clients switching from another preparer?**
The two most important items are the prior-year tax return and the prior-year adjusted gross income (AGI), which is required for e-filing verification. Ask new clients to either provide a copy of their previous return or authorize you to request an IRS transcript. Include this step early in the onboarding process so there is time to obtain the documents before the appointment.

**Can I onboard clients remotely, or do they need to come in person?**
Remote onboarding works well for most tax preparation clients and has become increasingly common. A digital intake form, secure document upload portal, and video appointment provide the same quality experience as in-person onboarding. Many preparers now offer a hybrid model where the initial consultation is virtual and document exchange is entirely digital, with in-person meetings available for clients who prefer them.

**What if a client does not complete their onboarding before the appointment?**
Automated reminders handle most cases, but some clients will show up underprepared regardless. The onboarding system should flag incomplete intakes so you know in advance which appointments may need extra time. For clients with significant gaps, a brief pre-appointment call to review what is missing can save time during the actual meeting.
